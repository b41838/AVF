Discussion Week 1

1. Less obvious perks of using 'live' data resources include logins and payments. How could Twitter be used in these two scenarios?

Logins is a very obvious resource to me, you see it everywhere now whether it’s to log in with Twitter, Facebook or Google+.  That is the greatest thing anyone has really ever thought of because it takes the burden of security off of the site/app developer and keeps it on the social network.  You also don’t need to bother your users with creating yet another account with another password.

A very cool service called “Pay With a Tweet” uses an individual tweet as a payment for a product or service.  You just put a “Pay With A Tweet” button on your website and when a user clicks on it, it posts to their Twitter feed, exposing all of their followers to your product.  According to their intro video there are 10,000 buttons that exist and they have generated over 400,000 downloads.  I don’t know how they actually make money but it’s a cool tool to help your content go viral.

I also found a few articles of companies, namely Ribbon and Flattr, who have been ‘asked’ to stop their use of the Twitter API because of a Terms of Use violation.  Flattr is a cool service that books them self as an online tip jar and used the ‘Favorite’ button as a way to tip someone.  First you bind your Flattr account to your Twitter account, fund your Flattr account then every time you ‘Favorite’ something on Twitter, it tips the other account (who would have already taken the same steps) a dime.  Twitter, however, said this is a form of paid advertising and thus violates their Terms of Use.

 

2. Visit Programmableweb.com and search for JSON-P data sources. Why would we need JSON-P, specifically?

If you want to host all of the data for an API you are releasing for public use, use JSONP.  JSON is an object notation format used for holding data and it works great, unless you don’t have access to the server it’s on, enter JSONP.  With JSONP you can build your API and keep full control of the data as client machines, be it a Desktop, Laptop, Phone, Tablet or other device can still go out and access your data.  As an app developer that’s gold, it’s one more way to build in dynamic content to your app.

4. Please describe any design and functionality intentions you may have for your Demo app components this month.

I intend to make my app a local art finding app where the intent is to snap photos of graffiti around town then alert your friends and the app community via Twitter, SMS (with your contacts), push notifications and mark it on a map with geolocation.  I’m not quite sure how to integrate instagram, I would like to be able to push the photos that a user takes to a public instagram feed but I don’t know if that’s possible.  Otherwise, I will be pulling in an instagram feed from either the local contemporary art museum, the Find A Way Collective or the Art Alley Artists Guild.

 

5. Briefly describe some past 'lessons learned' regarding workflow as it relates to research, design, development, etc.

I’ve learned an relearned many lessons in my career but the most important lesson is to not stare at a blank canvas.  This is important in any medium, whether you’re coding, designing in Photoshop, painting, drawing, etc.  Get something down, anything, because then you can start to build on it and generate that creative focused energy.  Also, know that your idea – no matter how amazing and unique… has probably already been done in one way or another.  AND THAT’S OK!  Do you think Facebook was the first social network?  Nope, it was arguably about six years late to the party and now it’s the biggest and best social network, ever.  Look for inspiration and build on it, don’t clone, improve.
